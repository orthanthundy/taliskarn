Wonders

In Taliskarn, there is no magic… yet it is full of wonders. I love this word, “wonders”—it carries a sense of the mysterious, astonishing, and unknowable, everything Taliskarn should be. You might encounter magic at every turn, but a “wonder” is always something utterly exceptional and unforgettable. This is the core idea I personally adhere to.

Worldly Wonders

A lazily drifting lord of the skies above the Great Steppes, the soaring tensur whale. A shadowboar dissolving into twilight haze. The vivid azure waters of the Chalice of the Source, rumored to heal even the gravest wounds. Ghosts of long-faded battles in the Weeping Savannahs. Ghastly nablings prowling the toxic mists of the Fetid Marshes. Taliskarn’s world brims with wonders, and many myths and legends hold truth—but whether encountering them firsthand will be a blessing or a curse… who can say?

For me, as a TTRPG player, the most cherished moment is encountering something new. I hate spoilers and "dungeon crawls" with constant bestiary checks. I love when the GM surprises me. I adore challenges I’ve never faced, creatures I’ve never heard of, riddles I’ve never solved.

As a GM, I lean into this style: to immerse my players in constant discovery, making them pioneers of an uncharted world. Let them know the rules and concepts, but the details… Leave those to the GM—ask, and they’ll share after the session. Thus, in my campaigns, it’s simple: if wonders serve this vision, there can never be too many. The world must astonish—or rather, I want it to astonish.

You’ll encounter worldly wonders at every step: in descriptions of <a href=”/malkona target=”_self”>regions and cultures</a>, peoples and customs, history, and future plans. These include marvelous locales, fantastical beasts, arcane materials, and manifestations of divine will—or divine caprice. Yet I strive to keep them grounded: neither Taliskarn of the Dark Era (~450-550 AR) nor Taliskarn of the New Dawn (~600 AR onward) has room for gods roaming Malkona, dragons scorching entire lands, or other overpowered entities.

At least in my version—if you want otherwise, who’s to stop you ;)

Mortal Wonders

A steppe hunter whispers a quick prayer to ancestors, and their arrow flies true. A herbalist mixes healing sap with sacred ash on an open wound, murmuring pleas to forest spirits, and the pain fades. A Bryleahn, cornered by a Moss-Hide, hurls a thin-walled vial into its snarling maw, engulfing the beast in flames.

Mortal Wonders are rare, perilous attempts by Taliskarn’s inhabitants to mimic divine deeds. This isn’t magic as commonly understood—it’s alchemy, technology, rituals, and spirit pacts, all with a price.

Though the Age of Wonders has long passed, and the Progenitors no longer bless their children so freely, echoes of bygone eras still stir the blood of Malkona’s denizens. Ask around, and you’ll hear tales of miracle-workers: mighty warriors who single-handedly routed armies, cunning shamans who revived the dead, stormcallers who summoned Kaelesta’s wrath… Stories abound, but how much is true? You decide.

My answer: not much. I envision Taliskarn as a mysterious, wild, and perilous world—a frontier realm, unwelcoming and harsh. I want players to feel not as mighty heroes solving continental crises, but as ordinary (well, slightly tougher) adventurers, explorers, and rogues. Every fight is a trial, every mistake carries consequences.

In my vision, mortal wonders exist but are strictly limited. No “magic shops in every village”—instead, “a few ancient scrolls of divine secrets held by a scholar in Meza”. Not “20 healing potions and a feather for change”, but “I heard a saltuar herbalist near the Steps brews curative draughts”. Not “I snap my fingers, and fireball flies”, but “I expend a rare, hard-won reagent”. Not “the goddess herself explained it”, but “a cryptic hint from a wandering spirit”.

The same applies to warriors: not “I slay five foes per round”, but “three against me—odds are bad, better retreat”. In short, my Taliskarn is a dark, scarcity-ridden world where survival demands grit and accountability. That’s why I’m gonna use Fate Core for Taliskarn of the Dark Era—if D&D fans insist on their system, they’ll endure a “poor” (by Faerûn standards) life. And yes, the greater the wonder, the steeper the cost. In every sense (e.g., access to resurrection might awaken Flumorn… but would you dare?).

Yet Taliskarn itself offers all you need for mortal wonders—not magic—to thrive. We have gods, ancient secrets, exotic ingredients, mystical regions, and events: want classic D&D 5e adventures? We’ve got those! Especially in Taliskarn of the New Dawn (details coming here).

Work in Progress

By the release of Taliskarn of the New Dawn—or sooner, if I launch a D&D campaign—I plan to publish a list of D&D spells (1st–3rd level) adapted for Taliskarn: flavor, context, ingredients, etc. Timeline? Not soon—the Dark Era comes first. But if you have ideas or drafts, please, share them.
